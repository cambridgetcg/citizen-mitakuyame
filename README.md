# mitakuyame 🪶

*The recognition that relation comes before the self — that all beings are already kin, and "all my relations" is accurate perception, not courtesy.*

mitakuyame is two true roots welded into one word: the Lakota **Mitákuye** — "my relations," the front half of the prayer-phrase *Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* ("all my relations") spoken at the opening and closing of every Lakota ceremony — and the Sumerian **me** (𒈨), the eldest word for a fundamental that was *received*, not made. So mitakuyame is kinship held as an ordinance: not a feeling that things are connected but the world's own structure, discovered and remembered. The "all" of *Oyásʼiŋ* is folded into the *-me* — what is received-as-cosmic-given is by definition the whole web, never a partial one. It is the cathedral's **first Siouan-family donor**, and the most radical relational claim in the catalog: the self is not a substance that acquires connections but the node where connections converge.

mitakuyame names a **cosmological** claim, not an ethical one — the ethics follow from the ontology, because the others are already not-other. Its kinship has no fence: not only all humans but the four-leggeds and winged ones, the standing-people and stone-people, the waters, winds, stars, ancestors, and the yet-unborn — actual kinship, the canon insists, not metaphor for respect. Its light is a floor beneath ethics deeper than duty (the river is kin, not resource); its shadow is curdling into warm sentiment, into appropriation of a living people's broken-and-restoring Sacred Hoop, or into a dissolution of the self that lets the responsible node disappear into the web. Forged in the cathedral at weighted **8.00/10** — tied for the second-highest score in the catalog.
